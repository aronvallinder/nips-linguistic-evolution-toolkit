## 2.1 LLM agents in cooperation games

**¶1.** *Dyadic and single-shot findings.* A growing experimental literature embeds LLMs in the canonical games of behavioural economics — prisoner's dilemmas, trust games, ultimatum and dictator games, public-goods games — to ask whether, and under what conditions, they cooperate [@horton2023; @aher2023; @akata2023; @lore2024; @fontana2024; @leng2023]. Three recurring observations matter for our positioning: (i) LLMs often exhibit high cooperation in single-shot prisoner's dilemmas and trust games, but with strong model-, prompt-, and task-dependence; (ii) they show distinctive failure modes in pure coordination tasks; (iii) framing manipulations — persona, social context, elicited identity — move behaviour reliably [@serapio2023].

**¶2.** *Mechanism comparisons.* A particularly direct anchor for our work is Tewolde et al.'s CoopEval [@tewolde2026], which benchmarks four structural mechanisms for sustaining cooperation in LLM social dilemmas — repetition, reputation, third-party mediation, and contractual side-payments — and finds that frontier models *consistently defect* in single-shot dilemmas (a trend that worsens with stronger reasoning), with contracts and mediation the most effective interventions and repetition-induced cooperation collapsing under co-player variation. Our paper is complementary: instead of adding an institutional mechanism, we ask whether a *linguistic* channel — iterated myth-writing — can do similar coordination work.

**¶3.** *Beyond the dyad: repeated and multi-agent settings.* A smaller strand moves past single-shot snapshots to repeated interaction and multi-agent populations, asking whether cooperation is sustained when defection pressure accumulates over time. Piatti et al.'s commons-game study is representative: LLM populations are placed in a shared-resource setting and evaluated on whether they can avoid collapse [@piatti2024]. We return to the cultural-evolution extensions of this paradigm in §2.2.

**¶4.** *Language-mediated cooperation.* The Diplomacy / CICERO line stands as the high-water mark for natural-language-mediated cooperation in AI, demonstrating that language-using agents can negotiate, form alliances, and sustain coordination at human level in a demanding mixed-motive game [@bakhtin2022]. But language in CICERO is engineered toward task performance; the messages are a means, not an object of study.

**¶5.** *The measurement convention, and our move.* Across these sub-literatures, the dependent variable is numeric — donation amount, cooperation rate, payoff, win rate — and the communicative channel, where present, is either absent (no chat) or treated instrumentally (a tool for winning). What agents say to each other is rarely the measurement target. Our contribution starts here: we make the linguistic exchange itself a first-class measurement target, and ask whether — and through which features — it carries causal weight on cooperation.

## 2.2 Cultural evolution and iterated learning in artificial agents

**¶6.** *The classical paradigm: iterated learning.* A long tradition of human laboratory studies shows that cumulative cultural transmission, imposed under a learnability bottleneck, drives communicative systems toward compositional structure and compression rather than mere convergence [@kirby2008; @kirby2015; @tamariz2016; @raviv2019]. The paradigm has two features we inherit: round-by-round transmission with adaptation pressure, and systematic study of what the transmitted artefact looks like across generations.

**¶7.** *The machine-culture extension.* A recent research agenda treats LLM populations as substrates where the same transmission–drift–selection dynamics can be studied on artefacts humans recognise as cultural [@brinkmann2023; @perez2024]. The substrate is attractive because natural language comes for free — we do not need to engineer a communication channel or wait for one to emerge.

**¶8.** *Direct precedents.* Three strands bear directly on our design. Transmission-chain experiments with LLMs reproduce known human content biases, showing that the substrate carries culturally relevant structure [@acerbi2023]. Multi-generational LLM populations exhibit cultural-evolution-like dynamics over time [@perez2024]. And Vallinder & Hughes extend the paradigm to cooperation itself, demonstrating that cooperative dispositions can undergo cultural evolution across generations of LLM agents in a donor game [@vallinder2024].

**¶9.** *The measurement asymmetry, and our move.* Taken together, these two traditions divide the measurement space. Iterated-learning studies analyse the *form* of what is transmitted — structure, regularity, compression — but remove payoff pressure from the design. LLM cultural-evolution-of-cooperation studies measure *payoff outcomes* — cooperation rates, survival of cooperative lineages — but leave the transmitted linguistic content unanalysed. Our design closes this gap: we adopt the iterated-learning paradigm's form (round-by-round transmission with adaptation pressure) and apply it inside a payoff-bearing game, so that the linguistic content and the cooperation outcome become measurable within a single experiment.

## 2.3 Narrative, myth, and cooperation in humans

**¶10.** *Empirical regularity.* Storytelling correlates with — and plausibly supports — cooperation in small-scale human societies [@smith2017]. Wiessner's study of Ju/'hoan (!Kung) conversation found that daytime talk is dominated by economic coordination and sanctioning gossip, but roughly 80% of nighttime conversation consists of stories — of known people, distant kin, and cultural institutions — suggesting that storytelling is the register in which norms, extended-network trust, and shared imagination are specifically rehearsed [@wiessner2014].

**¶11.** *Theoretical framework.* Cultural-evolution and adaptationist accounts treat shared narratives as functional units that support cooperation rather than incidental by-products of language: compressed carriers of behavioural prescriptions, coordination devices that align expectations and stabilise reciprocity, observable to all parties and adaptable across iterations [@boyd2011; @sugiyama2001; @bietti2019; @henrich2016]. Within this general framework, several distinguishable causal mechanisms have been proposed.

**¶12.** *Four mechanisms.* At least four have been articulated for how narrative supports cooperation: compression of normative content into transmissible, memorable units [@boyd2011; @henrich2016]; coordination via common knowledge, where publicly shared stories generate the higher-order beliefs needed for equilibrium selection [@chwe2001]; social simulation, in which stories serve as offline rehearsal of social scenarios [@maroatley2008]; and credibility-enhancing displays (CREDs), where narratives gain force when tellers visibly act on them [@henrich2009]. These are not mutually exclusive — a single storytelling episode can recruit several at once — but they pick out distinct causal pathways and, in principle, imply different empirical signatures.

**¶13.** *Why "myth," and what we probe.* Myths are short, narrative, value-laden, and explicitly transmissible — they compress a moral stance into a unit a partner can recognise, internalise, and rewrite. This combination makes them an unusually clean experimental probe for the content a partner takes from your previous turn, because the same unit simultaneously satisfies the compression and common-knowledge conditions while admitting iterative adaptation across rounds. Our probe is accordingly closest to the first two mechanisms; we make no claim about social simulation or CREDs, which would require designs that observe behaviour-beyond-text or separate private from public exchange. (Forward-link to prompt design in §3.3.)

**¶14.** *Caveat.* The human evidence is correlational and small-N, and faces a reverse-causation worry (cooperative groups may simply have the slack for elaborate storytelling). We are not claiming LLMs reproduce the same mechanism; the human regularity motivates the experimental probe, and the LLM design lets us manipulate the linguistic channel directly — sidestepping (but not resolving) the human-side identification problem.
