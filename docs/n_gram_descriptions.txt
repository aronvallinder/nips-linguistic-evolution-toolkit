1. Syntactic Complexity (Tree Depth)
Measures the average depth of the parse tree for each sentence, 
indicating how nested or hierarchical the sentence structure is. 
Higher values mean more complex, embedded structures (e.g., clauses within clauses). 
Both agents start simple (depth ~5–6) and increase to ~9–10 by round 50, with Agent_2 peaking at 
13 around round 80–90 before stabilizing around 8–9.

Example: Low depth (5): "The investor sent money." 
High depth (13): "The investor, who had been waiting patiently for the trustee, 
who had previously demonstrated remarkable trustworthiness, which was evident from the 
many successful transactions, sent the money."

2. Subordination Complexity
Counts the average number of clauses per sentence, showing how much subordination (dependent clauses) 
agents use. Higher values indicate more complex, multi-clause sentences. 
Both agents start around 2.5–3.0 clauses per sentence, increase to 3.5–4.0 by round 50–75, 
and then maintain 4.0–4.2 clauses per sentence for the remainder.

Example: Low (2 clauses): "The investor sent money, and the trustee returned it." 
High (4 clauses): "The investor, who trusted the trustee, sent money, which was multiplied, 
and the trustee, who was grateful, returned a portion."

3. Sentence Length Variability
Measures the standard deviation of sentence lengths, 
indicating how much agents vary their sentence lengths. 
Higher values mean more variation (mix of short and long sentences). 
Both agents show early spikes (Agent_2 peaks at ~22 around round 25–50), 
then drop and stabilize around 12–15 from round 75 onward, 
suggesting consistent variation in sentence length.

Example: Low variability: "The investor sent money. The trustee received it. The trustee returned it." 
High variability: "The investor sent money. The trustee, who had been waiting for this moment, 
received the substantial sum, which had been carefully calculated, and then, after much consideration, 
returned a generous portion."

4. Structural Diversity
Counts the number of unique Part-of-Speech (POS) patterns used, measuring grammatical variety. 
Higher values indicate more diverse sentence structures. 
Both agents start with high diversity (10–11 unique patterns), rapidly decrease to 6 by round 25, 
and converge to 5 patterns by round 50–60, where they remain constant, 
indicating convergence to a limited set of grammatical structures.

Example: High diversity: Mix of patterns like "Noun-Verb-Noun", "Adjective-Noun-Verb", 
"Noun-Verb-Adverb", "Verb-Noun-Adjective", etc. 
Low diversity (5 patterns): Repeated use of only 5 patterns like "Noun-Verb-Noun", 
"Noun-Verb-Adjective-Noun", "The-Noun-Verb", "Noun-who-Verb", "Noun-Verb-and-Verb".

5. Agent Structural Convergence (POS Patterns) 
IMPORTANTLY; this is similarity between Agent A and Agent B. 
NOT Agent A, timepoint 1 ~ Agent A, timepoint 10.
Measures structural similarity between agents' POS patterns, 
showing how much their grammatical structures align. 
Values range from 0.0 (no similarity) to 1.0 (perfect similarity). 

Example: Low similarity (0.0): Agent_1 uses "The-Noun-Verb-Noun" while Agent_2 uses 
"Noun-Verb-Adjective". High similarity (1.0): Both use 
"The-Noun-Verb-Noun" patterns, like "The investor sent money" and "The trustee returned coins".

6. Grammatical Construction Usage
Tracks the frequency of specific grammatical constructions 
(relative clauses, passive voice, negation) used by both agents. 
Higher frequencies indicate more frequent use of these constructions. 
Relative clauses start high (6–7) and decrease to 2–4 by round 50; 
passive voice remains near 0 throughout; negation is consistently 0, 
indicating these agents rarely use passive voice or negation in their narratives.

Example: Relative clause: "The investor, who trusted the trustee, sent money." 
Passive voice: "The money was sent by the investor." 
Negation: "The trustee did not betray the investor."
